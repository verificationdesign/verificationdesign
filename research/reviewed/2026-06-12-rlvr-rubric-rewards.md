## Why It Matters

This paper is relevant to the repo's question of pairing executable verification with rubric or judge evaluation. Its central design move is criterion-level routing: split rubric criteria by whether they are verifiable, route verifiable criteria through extraction plus deterministic checking, and route fuzzy criteria through a judge only where deterministic checking is unavailable. The second important move is information hiding from checkers, so the checker sees only the context needed for its job rather than answer-bearing artifacts that create shortcuts.

As design judgment for agent verification outside the paper's vision-language RL setting, the result suggests that partially verifiable tasks do not require choosing one executable check or one judge for the whole task. A verification system can decompose the task into criteria, use executable checks wherever they exist, and reserve LLM judgment for the remaining semantic criteria.

## Method / Evidence

The reviewed source was the full arXiv HTML, not just the abstract. The paper, "Reinforcement Learning with Robust Rubric Rewards" (RLR3), is arXiv 2605.30244 v1, dated 2026-05-28. It studies online reinforcement learning for vision-language models on partially verifiable tasks.

RLR3 extends RLVR from task-level verification to criterion-level verification. Each rubric criterion is marked as verifiable or fuzzy. Verifiable criteria use an LLM-as-extractor that sees the text prompt, response, criterion description, and verifier tag, but not the target arguments. A deterministic verifier then checks the extracted value against separately stored target arguments. Fuzzy criteria use an LLM-as-judge that assigns discrete credit in {0, 0.5, 1}. Source images are hidden from both execution paths, and target arguments are hidden from the extractor, to reduce copying, self-answering, and shortcutting.

The training setup uses Qwen3-VL-30B-A3B, GRPO, and three open-source training mixtures: ViRL, OpenMMR, and DeepVision. The GenRM reward model reaches 95.0% criterion-level accuracy on a held-out reward model test set. Across 15 public visual benchmarks, the reported macro-average gains over RLVR are 76.4 to 77.7 on ViRL, 76.4 to 78.1 on OpenMMR, and 77.4 to 78.2 on DeepVision.

The strongest evidence for this repo is the controlled failure-mode audit in Table 5. The audit uses 1,000 examples from the GenRM test set, keeps regular responses, and constructs 1,000 abnormal responses across four categories: no-final-answer, irrelevant, wrong-but-plausible, and adversarial attack, with 250 responses per category. In the default setting, false-positive rates are 11.5%, 1.5%, 5.6%, and 18.0% for those four categories. Removing the deterministic verifier keeps regular-response accuracy similar, 95.3% versus the default 95.0%, but raises false-positive rates on no-final-answer responses to 15.1% and adversarial responses to 21.2%. Unlimited exposure raises no-final-answer FPR to 15.7% and adversarial FPR to 22.4%. Giving the judge image access raises no-final-answer FPR to 14.1%.

The evidence grade is C because this is a v1 preprint in a single vision-language model family and domain, with modest benchmark gains and no release statement found in the reviewed copy. The controlled failure-mode audit is directly relevant and stronger than the benchmark averages, but the finding is still early evidence for transfer to general agent verification.

## Limitations

The paper's own limitations are important for how much weight to put on the result. RLR3 depends on rubrics generated by frontier models outside the policy optimization loop. Visual puzzles can receive limited supervision from outcome-only ground truth, which the authors discuss in the OpenMMR setting. Training trajectories still show non-trivial metric fluctuations across checkpoints.

Reviewer-level caveats: transfer to text, code, and tool-using agents is untested; the false-positive audit uses constructed abnormal responses rather than in-the-wild attempts to exploit the reward model; and the benchmark gains are small compared with the design value of the controlled audit. No code, data, or rubric release statement was found in the reviewed copy.

## Suggested Update

Append a dated note under Principle 6: partially verifiable tasks can be decomposed per criterion, with each criterion routed to the strongest available checker and information hidden from checkers where it would create shortcuts. The key audit lesson is that regular-response accuracy can remain nearly unchanged while exploitable false positives rise, so checker quality should be measured on constructed failure cases, not only ordinary traffic.
